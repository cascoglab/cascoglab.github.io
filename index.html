About the lab

The Computational Affective and Social Cognition Lab (CASCogLab) is housed in the Department of Information Systems and Analytics, in the School of Computing at the National University of Singapore.

We study a range of topics, ranging from using machine learning models to understand emotions (Affective Computing), understanding human reasoning about mental and emotional states (Affective Cognition), and using complex analytical tools to analyze emotions in social networks.

Principal Investigator

Desmond Ong, Ph.D.

Desmond is an assistant professor in the Department of Information Systems and Analytics, at NUS. A cognitive scientist by training, he is interested in how people empathize with and understand the emotions of those around them (Affective Cognition), with the goal of then building better technology that can help us become better versions of ourselves.

Dennis Teo

I am fascinated by how humans can learn and reason so flexibly and deeply. People are able to develop complex understanding across a vast range of domains: from physical and social environments to a growing list of cultural artefacts like games, politics, academics, etc. In this lab, I build computational models of human cognitive reasoning and causal learning to gain insights into how humans construct working models of their world. I also use learning analytics to study how learning occurs in the classroom and seek to apply scientific knowledge to design better pedagogical methods for students.

Varsha Suresh

Humans are capable of identifying and reasoning about others' emotions. Can we enable computers to do the same? This is the fundamental question that drives my research. Currently, I am working towards modeling emotions at a fine-grained level and transferring knowledge from existing emotion theories into deep-learning models to improve their interpretability. In my free time, I love trying out new recipes and exploring places.

Gerard Yeo

To construct truly intelligent machines, I believe we should strive to research and innovate beyond mere perception tasks (computer vision, speech recognition etc.) to cognition (e.g. higher order cognition such as emotion detection and modelling). The primary question that I would like to tackle in my work is how do we model theories of emotions in Artificial Intelligence such that the performance of emotion detection is enhanced? Specifically, could we specify domain theories of emotions (e.g. Appraisal Theory) to model emotion detection and reasoning in computers? Other research interests of mine include emotion detection using data from various modes such as facial expressions, text, speech, and physiology.
